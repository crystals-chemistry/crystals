Natural & Synthetic Crystals.

Natural Crystals

Physical Properties

Natural crystals/gemstones are a collection of minerals that are naturally occurring. They are inorganic – mined from the ground and not made through any organic processes. Crystals are solid at room temperature and have a defined chemical composition. The atoms in a gemstone are arranged in a systematic and repeating pattern, which gives them a very ordered internal structure. As a result, gemstones are typically quite durable and hard to break, landing them higher than a 6 on the Mohs scale of mineral hardness.

Beauty

The beauty of a gem is measured in terms of clarity, brilliance, luster, colour, and rarity. Gemstones are divided into two specific classes on this basis – precious gems and semi precious gems. Precious gems are diamonds, rubies, sapphires, and emeralds, all of which are extremely expensive and highly sought after. Any gems that aren’t classified as precious are considered semi-precious. The value of a gem is also decided based on these factors, and it also depends on the size/weight, which is measured in carats.

Cut

Gemstones are cut to enhance their beauty. A faceted cut is a type of cut on gemstones where it has been shaped to have flat cut surfaces, and the overall shape can be round, oval, square, rectangular, or pear-shaped. This type of cut is preferred for transparent gemstones. A cabochon cut is a type of cut on gemstones where it has been shaped to have a smooth and rounded top and a flat base. This type of cut is mainly used for opaque or translucent gemstones.

Structures, Forces & Bonding

There are 2 main structures of crystals: covalent network crystals, and ionic crystals.

Covalent Network Crystals

In covalent network crystals, macromolecules of atoms are held together by covalent bonds. In this type of crystal, intermolecular bonds are synonymous with intramolecular bonds because the atoms bond individually with one another to form the whole structure (there is no division of molecules). These types of crystals have strong covalent bonding, meaning that they have arranged organized arrays, high melting points, and have negligible solubility in polar solvents. In addition, they are not electrically conductive because the electrons used in bonding are not free to move throughout the structure. An example of this type of crystal is a diamond.

Ionic Crystals

Ionic crystals are formed by ionic bonding – metal cations and non-metal anions. The ions are arranged in an ionic lattice to form regular repeating patterns. These crystals have a relatively high melting point and have the ability to dissolve in polar solvents. The localized electrons in ionic crystals limit the electrical conductivity. Examples of this type of crystal are ruby and sapphire.

Formation of Crystals

Most gemstones form in Earth’s crust, however diamond and peridot form in the mantle. However, regardless of where they are
formed, all gemstones are mined in the crust. The Earth’s crust is made of igneous, sedimentary, and metamorphic rock.

Igneous Rock

In igneous processes, magma rises from the Earth’s mantle to its crust through volcanic pipes. The magma that does not reach the surface will cool down slowly, and crystallize to form minerals. The change in pressure will cause chemical exchanges between the magma and the surrounding rock. Quartz, Emerald, Garnet, and Diamond are all gems that come from igneous rock.

Sedimentary Rock

Erosion and weathering causes igneous rock that reaches the surface of the Earth to break down into smaller particles. Layers of sediments settle on land or underwater after being carried by wind or water. Chemical and physical changes like lithification are undergone due to compaction, which leads to the creation of sedimentary rock. Examples of gemstones that are associated with sedimentary rock include Jasper, Malachite, Opal and Zircon.

Metamorphic Rock

Tectonic plate interactions and the presence of intrusive magma in an area produce heat and pressure changes. Igneous and sedimentary rocks nearby transform in terms of chemistry and crystal structure to create metamorphic rock. Examples of gemstones associated with metamorphic rock include Beryl, Jade, Turquoise, Ruby and Sapphire.

Synthetic Crystals

What Are Synthetic Crystals?

Contrary to popular belief, hearing the word “synthetic” in front of gemstones does not immediately make them of lesser quality or “fake”. Rather synthetic gemstones created in a lab are produced to virtually mimic the chemical composition, structure, optical properties and physical composition of natural gemstones. Although in some cases such as opal, additional properties are present when created synthetically.

History of Synthetic Crystals

Records show that the ancient Egyptians used coloured glass to mimic the beautiful crystals found in nature; including lapis, turquoise and coral. Similar mediums were used up until the 19th century, when flame fusion was discovered by French chemist Auguste Verneuil in 1883.

Examples of Synthetic Crystals

Alexandrite

Synthetic alexandrites are somewhat rare, and come in incandescent and daylight colours. Alexandrite is formed through various processes including: crystal pulling and flux growth.

Quartz

Quartz are beautiful gemstones that have many uses in technology, due to the fact that they can be used to generate an electric current under pressure and can vibrate in precise response to alternating current. These properties allow them to be used in watches, clocks, oscillators, communications equipment, and filters.

Corundum (Ruby & Sapphire)

Synthetic corundum, which includes ruby and sapphire, can be made through flux growth and flame fusion processes. Because these processes are becoming more commercially available, the prices for synthetic corundum can vary greatly, from very affordable to quite expensive.

Opal

Opal is formed differently compared to most other gemstones. White other gem materials are grown from single crystals, synthetic opals are created by setting millions of spheres of identical size into perfect arrays and binding them together using a polymer resin, which is not found in natural opal. This lengthy process can take up to a year to perfect the gem.

Beryl

Synthetic beryl can be produced in many colors including yellow, red, blue and green. The most well known beryl is the emerald form. Emerald in was created in the late 1930’s through the flux growth method. Also, Russia became a significant producer of synthetic beryls ever since the late 1980’s and 1990’s.

Spinel

When scientists were trying to create synthetic sapphire, they ended up creating synthetic blue spinel by accident. Ever since, synthetic spinel has been commonly used as a substitute for many natural gems, as they can be found in any colour. Synthetic spinels can be grown using In the flux-growth method and the older flame-fusion method.

Natural vs Synthetic Crystals

Formation

Synthetic gemstones are lab-grown, whereas natural gemstones are mainly formed and mined from the Earth’s crust.

Colour

Synthetic gemstones will have a more vivid colour due to being. lab grown, as they have less impurities impacting them.

Availability

Due to being grown in a lab, there is an unlimited amount of synthetic gemstones. However, natural gemstones are more rare.

Value

Synthetic gemstones are valued at a significantly lower price than natural gemstones due to the higher rarity of natural gemstones.

Spectroscopic Analysis

Spectroscopy is used for detecting synthetic gemstones, as well as for identifying the origin of the stone and if it was in a conflicted area. The study emerged after Isaac Newton attempted to separate rays of sunlight into colours with the help of a prism. Later on, in the mid 1800’s, researchers began to map out the solar spectrum produced from the sun, using it to detect the absorption spectra for the purpose of finding common elements present in gemstones.

Researchers conducted a study using 22 synthetic and 19 natural faceted gems, using Visible/Near-Infrared spectroscopy for identification of the gemstones. The results show that the refractive indexes of the synthetic gems ranged from 1.560 to 1.580 while the natural gems had refractive indexes from 1.571 to 1.598.
