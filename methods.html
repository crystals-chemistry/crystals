Methods of Creating Crystals.

There are many different methods of creating synthetic crystals. Most of these methods fall into two categories: melt processes and solution processes.

Melt Processes

Flame Fusion (Verneuil Process)

This method was perfected by French Chemist August Verneuil, and is often called the Verneuil method. It is the least expensive and most common way to make gems such as synthetic corundum (ruby) or spinel.

The process involves dropping powdered chemicals through a high-temperature flame, where it melts and falls into a rotating pedestal to produce a synthetic crystal. For creating synthetic ruby, powdered Al2O3 and a coloring agent such as chromium are melted as they are dropped into the high-temperature flame. The apparatus used for this process is similar to a blow torch.

Crystal Pulling (Czochralski Process)

This method was invented by Polish Chemist Jan Czochralski. This method is used in the production of ruby, alexandrite, and YAG and GGG (types of synthetic diamond.) This method creates very large and pure crystals.

In this process, a melt is prepared in a heat-resistant crucible. A seed crystal is lowered into it. The temperature is reduced to slightly above the melting point of the seed, and the seed is then rotated and slowly lifted out of the melt.

Solution Processes

Flux Growth Process

This method is used to produce synthetic gems including emerald, ruby, alexandrite, and GGG (a type of synthetic diamond). Growing synthetic crystals using this method can take up to a year, and the equipment for this method is very expensive.

Crystals are grown in a crucible that contains the composition of the mineral to be grown and flux. Flux is a solid material which, when melted, dissolves other materials the same way that water dissolves sugar. When the dissolved chemical solution cools, synthetic crystals form. However, if it cools completely, the gems would be encased in the flux. Due to this, the flux is poured off or drained before it solidifies. It is also important to cool the solution very slowly to grow as large of a crystal as possible.

Hydrothermal Growth Process

This method is very slow and expensive, however, it is the only method for growing synthetic quartz. This method uses a high temperature and pressure vessel that acts like a pressure cooker, called an autoclave, where a nutrient is supplied along with water. A temperature gradient is maintained between the opposite ends of the chamber. At the hotter end, the nutrient solute dissolved. At the cooler end, it is deposited on a seed crystal, growing the desired crystal.
