Diamonds.

How Diamonds Are Made

How Natural Diamonds Are Made

Natural diamonds used today were formed over 3 billion years ago deep within the Earth’s crust in the mantle- diamonds are found at a depth of approximately 150-200km below the surface (900-1,300 degrees Celsius) and at a pressure of 45 to 60 kilobars (50,000 times that of atmospheric pressure on the Earth’s surface). Diamonds in the mantle form through a metasomatic process where fluid made up of carbon, oxygen, hydrogen, nitrogen and sulfur (the exact chemical formula is unknown) is produced by reduction of oxidized carbon (e.g., CO2 or CO3) or oxidation of a reduced phase such as methane.

Under these conditions, molten lamproite and kimberlite (magma) are also formed within the Earth’s upper mantle and expand at a rapid rate. This expansion causes the magma to erupt, forcing it to the Earth’s surface and taking along with it diamond bearing rocks. Moving at an incredible speed, the magma takes the path with least resistance, forming a ‘pipe’ to the surface. As it cools the magma hardens to form Kimberlite and settles in vertical structures known as kimberlite pipes. These kimberlite pipes are the most significant source of diamonds, yet it is estimated that only 1 in every 200 kimberlite pipes contain gem-quality diamonds.

How Lab-Grown Diamonds Are Made

To create lab-grown diamonds, a diamond seed must first be selected- a diamond seed is a hair thin sheet of preexisting diamond with no cracks or other imperfections. Scientists then take multiple ‘seeds’ and place them into a metal chamber. Using pipes hydrogen gas (H2) and methane (CH4) is pumped into the chamber simultaneously to heat and pressure being added. When the machine heats the diamond fragments and gasses to thousands of degrees celsius (about 1,100 degrees celsius) at just the right pressure (about 50 kilobars), methane undergoes a decomposition reaction and separates into carbon and hydrogen gas. A plasma ball of hydrogen is created inside the chamber, this further speeds up the rate of reaction. The carbon is then deposited on the seeds which build the diamonds, this simulates the natural process but speeds it up, and in a few weeks a lab diamond is created. Using lasers and grinders the diamonds are smoothed and shaped into the classic gem appearance. The process is not easy and the technology is expensive but overall is cheaper than natural diamonds.

What is the difference between natural and lab-grown diamonds?

Natural diamonds and lab grown diamonds are virtually indistinguishable. The chemical composition and structure of the diamonds are identical, some natural diamonds will have trace amounts of nitrogen in them however this small difference is not easily observed and the regular person will not be able to tell the two apart. Both are real diamonds and they both sparkle, the major difference is in the price and sustainability. Natural diamonds are hard to come across and with our current use of diamonds they are used up much more quickly than they are naturally formed, while lab grown diamonds can be made within weeks and are much cheaper than harvesting natural diamonds.

Diamonds in Space

Although diamonds on Earth are rare, they are very common in space. In meteorites, about three percent of the carbon is in the form of nanodiamonds. Small diamonds are able to form in the cold of space because their lower surface energy makes them more stable than graphite. The isotopic signatures of some nanodiamonds indicate they were formed outside the Solar System in stars. High pressure experiments predict that large quantities of diamonds condense from methane into a diamond rain on the ice giant planets Uranus and Neptune. Some extrasolar planets may be almost entirely composed of diamond. Diamonds may exist in carbon-rich stars, particularly white dwarfs.

Diamond Reactions

Due to its chemical structure and strong covalent bonds at normal temperatures and pressure a diamond is one of the strongest materials on Earth. However just like any material when heated to high enough temperatures the bonds begin to weaken, at about 763° Celsius diamonds will vaporize. At these high temperatures, the diamond will undergo oxidation. Diamonds are a pure form of carbon so when they interact with oxygen at high enough temperatures, carbon oxidizes forming carbon dioxide (CO2) and carbon monoxide (CO).

Diamond vs. Graphite

Both diamond and graphite are pure carbon. Yet diamond is the hardest mineral known, and graphite is very soft.

Diamond is an electrical insulator while graphite is a good conductor of electricity, diamonds are usually transparent, but graphite is opaque. The reason why they are so different lies in their atomic structures. Graphite forms in sheets where the carbon atoms have strong bonds on the same layer, but only weak bonds to the layer above or below. On the other hand, the carbon atoms in diamond have strong bonds in three dimensions. In diamond, the atoms are very closely packed and each atom is connected to four other carbon atoms, giving it a very strong and rigid structure in three dimensions. The differences between diamond and graphite highlight the importance of crystal structure to the properties of a gemstone. For example, all gems that have a cubic crystal habit, where the crystal structure is completely symmetrical, have an important property in common. Cubic solids refract light in all directions at the same velocity. So even though diamond, garnet and spinel have quite different chemical compositions, they are all singly refractive because of their similar crystal structure. Other less symmetrical crystal groups are doubly refractive, including orthorhombic, monoclinic and triclinic, all of which have specific internal symmetries and axes that dictate how light bends as it enters the crystal.
